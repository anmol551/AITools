CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
------------------------
1. CONCLUSION
----------------------------------------
The research centres on a dual-task financial risk prediction pipeline built from a synthetic loan dataset containing 20,000 records. This pipeline targets two goals: predicting a continuous risk score through regression and deriving a binary loan approval classification using a median threshold on those scores. It begins with dataset analysis, followed by categorical encoding and feature selection through Recursive Feature Elimination with a Random Forest estimator. Six domain-specific features, such as Income Stability Ratio and Debt Stress Index, are engineered alongside ten selected base features before scaling. Three models of increasing complexity—ElasticNet, a 1D Convolutional Neural Network, and TabNet, an attention-based deep learning architecture for tabular data—are trained on an 80/20 train-test split. TabNet emerges as the top performer, recording an MAE of 1.8953, R² of 0.8839, classification accuracy of 89.1%, and ROC-AUC of 0.9598, excelling in both regression and classification tasks.

A key aspect of this work is the unified approach where a single regressor handles continuous risk prediction, and a threshold converts outputs into binary decisions. This avoids the complexity of separate models for each task, ensuring consistent results between risk scoring and approval outcomes. TabNet’s design, with attention-driven feature selection across five sequential steps, offers built-in interpretability, vital for financial contexts requiring transparency. A virtual batch size of 128 within larger batches of 512 aids stability during training on tabular data, addressing common challenges in smaller batch scenarios.

Beyond modelling, the pipeline integrates a Streamlit web application that uses the trained TabNet model and scaler for real-time predictions. It processes user inputs, applies live feature engineering, generates risk scores, and enforces a business rule where scores above 50 lead to rejection. An OpenAI GPT-4o-mini API call then crafts a detailed underwriting report covering risk analysis and financial insights. The engineered features embed domain knowledge directly into the input space, easing the model’s learning task. A post-prediction governance layer further aligns outputs with lending policies, adding a practical dimension to the system. This blend of deep learning with language model explanations transforms numerical predictions into clear, compliance-focused reports for decision-making and audits.

2. FUTURE WORK
----------------------------------------
Future research in this domain can explore several avenues to refine the predictive models and broaden their practical application. One key area lies in testing different machine learning and deep learning architectures beyond the current ElasticNet, CNN, and TabNet setups. Experimenting with models such as gradient boosting or transformer-based structures could uncover better performance for complex patterns in loan data. Integrating diverse data sources like social media metrics or real-time financial transactions might also enrich the input features. Refining how features are chosen, focusing on clearer interpretability, could help in understanding model decisions better. Adopting advanced validation techniques, such as cross-validation with stratified sampling, may ensure more reliable outcomes across varied datasets.

Another direction involves examining how different preprocessing steps affect model accuracy. Testing various methods to handle data imbalance or scaling approaches could reveal optimal strategies for preparing loan data. Applying these models to real-world settings, such as banking or fintech platforms, would test their practical value and scalability in dynamic environments. Ethical considerations must also be prioritised, ensuring fairness in predictions across diverse applicant groups. Investigating bias in features like income or credit history could lead to more equitable systems that avoid unfair denials or approvals.

Expanding on these ideas, future studies might delve into hybrid models combining elements of convolutional networks and attention mechanisms to capture intricate data relationships. Exploring temporal data trends, if available, could add a dynamic layer to risk assessment. Industry partnerships might facilitate access to larger datasets for testing model stability under different economic conditions. Addressing privacy concerns while using external data sources will be vital to maintain trust. Developing guidelines for transparent model outputs can aid stakeholders in decision-making processes. These steps aim to build on the current findings, pushing towards more accurate and fair predictive tools in financial assessments for loan approvals.
