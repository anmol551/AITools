### Methodology

#### Text Classification Design for AI vs. Human Detection

This research focuses on designing a framework to distinguish between AI-generated and human-written text through a structured approach. The pipeline begins with an extensive literature survey to understand existing methods and gaps in text classification. Data collection follows, sourcing a large dataset of essays for analysis. Preprocessing steps clean and prepare the text for feature extraction, ensuring quality input for model training. Various deep learning models, including DistilBERT, FocusCNN, AttentionCNN, and Context RF, are trained on the processed data to capture linguistic patterns. After training, the models undergo testing to assess their ability to classify text accurately. A web application, named TextOrigin App, is developed using Dash to provide an interactive platform for users to input text and receive classification results, ensuring practical application of the research.

The entire process integrates multiple stages to build a comprehensive system for text detection. Data is split into training, validation, and test sets to maintain balance during model learning. Different architectures are explored to identify effective strategies for distinguishing text origins. The pipeline also includes visualisation tools to study linguistic differences between AI and human text. Testing of the TextOrigin App ensures user accessibility and accurate delivery of predictions, forming a complete cycle from data handling to real-world deployment.

#### Dataset Overview for Text Classification

The dataset used in this research comprises around 500,000 essays, split between AI-generated and human-written content. Sourced from multiple platforms and compiled by Shane Gerami, it offers a diverse collection of text samples. The data shape stands at 487,235 records, with 62.8% human-written and 37.2% AI-generated entries, providing a balanced yet challenging set for classification tasks. This large volume and mix of origins make it suitable for training deep learning models to detect subtle differences in writing styles.

Selection of this dataset was driven by its extensive coverage of text types and sources, ensuring a broad representation of linguistic patterns. It supports the goal of building a model capable of handling varied inputs in real-world scenarios.

Ethical aspects were considered during data usage, ensuring no personal information is exposed. The dataset is publicly available, and proper attribution is given to the compiler. Source: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/shanegerami/ai-vs-human-text

Key data representations are as follows:
- Total records: 487,235
- Human text: 62.8%
- AI text: 37.2%
- Primary format: Textual essays

#### Text Data Preparation

Preparing text data is vital for ensuring accurate classification of AI-generated versus human-written content.

##### Text Cleaning

Text cleaning involves removing irrelevant elements from raw text to create a consistent input for models. This step targets noise such as punctuation, special characters, and unnecessary spaces that might interfere with pattern recognition. By standardising the text, it becomes easier for algorithms to focus on meaningful content. The choice of cleaning stems from its ability to reduce complexity in data, especially in a dataset with diverse writing styles like essays. A study by Kathuria et al. (2020) suggests that eliminating such noise significantly boosts the clarity of input for machine learning tasks.

This process directly improves data quality by creating uniformity across samples. It helps models concentrate on linguistic structures rather than irrelevant symbols or formatting issues. Cleaning also reduces the computational load by trimming down unnecessary data points, allowing faster processing. For a task like distinguishing AI from human text, where subtle cues matter, a clean dataset lays the foundation for effective feature extraction and classification.

##### Stopword Removal

Stopword removal refers to excluding common words like "the" or "and" that carry little unique information. These words often dominate text frequency but offer minimal value in distinguishing content origins. This technique was chosen for its capacity to sharpen focus on meaningful vocabulary, crucial for detecting stylistic differences in AI and human writing. As shown by Ladani and Desai (2020), trimming such words enhances the signal-to-noise ratio in text analysis.

Applying this step improves model performance by reducing data dimensionality, which speeds up training and cuts down on irrelevant noise. It allows algorithms to prioritise content-rich terms that reflect authorship traits. In the context of text classification, this ensures that the model captures distinctive patterns rather than generic fillers. The impact is a more efficient and focused analysis, aligning with the need to discern subtle differences in writing styles.

##### Lemmatisation

Lemmatisation transforms words into their base or root form, such as changing "running" to "run". It aims to group related terms under a single representation for consistent analysis. This method was selected to handle variations in word forms often seen in essays, ensuring that models treat similar meanings as one entity. According to Pant et al. (2024), this approach aids in reducing vocabulary size while preserving semantic value.

The benefit lies in enhancing data coherence, which directly supports better model learning. By consolidating word variants, it minimises redundancy and strengthens the focus on core content. This is particularly useful in distinguishing AI from human text, where word choice patterns are key. Lemmatisation thus refines the dataset, enabling algorithms to detect underlying structures more effectively and improving overall classification accuracy.

#### Architecture Development for Text Detection

Model building forms a critical stage in creating an effective system for classifying AI-generated versus human-written text.

##### DistilBERT

DistilBERT is a compact version of the BERT model designed for natural language processing tasks. It retains much of BERT's capability while being lighter and faster, making it suitable for text classification challenges like detecting AI versus human writing. This model was chosen for its balance between performance and resource efficiency, ideal for handling a large dataset of essays. Its architecture excels at understanding contextual relationships in text, which is essential for capturing subtle stylistic differences. As noted by Kici et al. (2021), DistilBERT offers a practical solution for complex language tasks without heavy computational demands.

Internally, DistilBERT operates on a transformer-based structure with multiple layers of attention mechanisms. It processes text through token embeddings and positional encodings to grasp word relationships across sentences. Key parameters include the number of layers, attention heads, and hidden size, which are tuned to optimise learning. The model breaks down input into smaller units, applying self-attention to weigh word importance in context. This mechanism ensures a deep grasp of linguistic nuances, vital for accurate text origin classification.

##### FocusCNN

FocusCNN is a convolutional neural network variant adapted for text analysis, emphasising specific patterns in data. It was selected for this research due to its strength in identifying local features like phrase structures, which differ between AI and human text. This model suits the task by focusing on targeted segments of essays, picking up on repetitive or formulaic patterns often seen in machine-generated content. A study by Zhang et al. (2023) affirms that such networks excel in tasks requiring detailed feature capture from sequential data.

The working principle of FocusCNN relies on convolutional layers that scan text for patterns using filters of varying sizes. These filters detect key textual elements, passing them through pooling layers to reduce dimensionality while retaining critical information. Parameters like filter count and kernel size shape its ability to spot relevant cues. The architecture processes input as a sequence, transforming it into feature maps that highlight significant textual traits, aiding in precise classification of text origins.

##### AttentionCNN

AttentionCNN combines convolutional networks with attention mechanisms to prioritise important text segments. This approach was picked for its ability to focus on relevant parts of essays, crucial for distinguishing writing styles between AI and human authors. It addresses the need to weigh certain words or phrases more heavily, especially in longer texts where context matters. According to Sufian et al. (2022), integrating attention with convolution enhances the model's capacity to interpret complex language patterns effectively.

At its core, AttentionCNN uses convolutional layers to extract local features, followed by an attention layer that assigns weights to different text portions based on their significance. Parameters such as attention heads and filter dimensions guide its focus. The model processes input through a series of operations, generating feature representations that are refined by attention scores. This dual structure ensures a balanced analysis of both local and global text elements, supporting accurate detection of authorship differences.

##### Context RF

Context RF refers to a random forest model adapted to consider contextual text features for classification. It was chosen for this study due to its effectiveness in handling structured data derived from text embeddings, fitting the goal of classifying AI versus human content. This method shines in interpreting high-dimensional data, common in language tasks, by building decision trees based on feature interactions. As highlighted by Paul et al. (2022), random forests provide strong performance in diverse classification scenarios.

The model operates by constructing multiple decision trees, each trained on random subsets of features extracted from text. It aggregates predictions through majority voting to determine the final class. Key parameters include tree depth and the number of trees, which influence its decision-making process. Context RF analyses input features holistically, capturing relationships among textual elements. This approach ensures a comprehensive evaluation of data, aiding in distinguishing subtle differences in text origin with high precision.

#### Assessment Metrics for Model Performance

Evaluating model performance is essential to measure the effectiveness of text classification systems.

Accuracy represents the proportion of correct predictions made by a model out of all predictions. It serves as a primary metric in this research due to its straightforward interpretation of overall success in classifying AI versus human text. A high value indicates reliable detection across the dataset, making it a key indicator for model comparison.

Precision measures the ratio of true positive predictions to the total positive predictions. This metric holds importance in ensuring that identified AI or human texts are correctly labelled, minimising false positives. It was chosen to reflect the model's trustworthiness in specific classifications, vital for practical applications.

Recall calculates the ratio of true positives to all actual positives in the data. It assesses the model's ability to identify all relevant instances, crucial for not missing AI or human texts. This metric suits the need to ensure comprehensive detection in unbalanced datasets.

F1 Score combines precision and recall into a single value, balancing both aspects. It was selected to provide a harmonised view of model performance, especially useful when dealing with varying class distributions in text data.

Hamming Loss evaluates the fraction of incorrect labels in multi-label settings. Though less common, it offers insight into specific errors, aiding in fine-tuning models for complex text classification tasks.

#### Explainable AI Approach for Text Detection

Explainable AI, or XAI, refers to methods that make machine learning decisions transparent and understandable to users. In the context of distinguishing AI-generated from human-written text, XAI is vital to clarify how models arrive at specific classifications. Techniques like SHAP and LIME were adopted to provide insights into feature importance and decision processes. The selection of these methods stems from their ability to break down complex model outputs into interpretable components, ensuring trust in predictions. As per the research done by Safder et al. (2020), SHAP values effectively highlight which text elements influence outcomes most.

Transparency in model decisions builds confidence among users, especially in applications like text classification where biases can mislead results. LIME, focusing on local explanations, complements SHAP by offering case-specific insights. According to a study by Jiang et al. (2021), LIME helps in understanding individual predictions by approximating model behaviour around specific inputs. These approaches together ensure a deeper grasp of model logic, addressing ethical concerns and supporting user acceptance. By integrating such tools, the research aims to bridge the gap between complex algorithms and practical comprehension, making the classification process accessible and justifiable to a wider audience.

#### TextOrigin App Development Framework

Dash was chosen for its simplicity in creating interactive interfaces with Python, aligning with the research goal of delivering an accessible tool for text analysis. Its flexibility allows seamless integration of machine learning models, making it ideal for real-time predictions. As noted by researchers like Siegelman et al. (2022), Dash supports rapid development of data-driven applications with minimal coding overhead.

The benefits of Dash include its user-friendly design, enabling non-technical users to input text and view results effortlessly. The interface features clear visuals like confidence bars, enhancing readability of predictions. It also supports quick feedback, vital for iterative testing by end-users. According to a survey by Pacifici et al. (2020), such frameworks improve engagement by simplifying complex outputs. TextOrigin App thus ensures that classification results are not only accurate but also presented in an intuitive format, encouraging broader adoption. This focus on usability extends the research impact, allowing diverse audiences to interact with the system without needing deep technical knowledge, thereby bridging the gap between advanced models and practical application.
