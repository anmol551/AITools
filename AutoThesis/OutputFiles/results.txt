The findings from this study on loan risk prediction reveal intriguing patterns. The outcomes of this research carry important weight for financial sectors dealing with loan approvals. The high accuracy of TabNet at 89.10% suggests it can support banks in making better decisions on loan applications by predicting risk scores with clarity. For businesses adopting this technology could improve efficiency and reduce losses from bad loans but must balance this with transparency to maintain client confidence. Weighing these benefits against potential drawbacks is vital for responsible use in real-world settings. The findings offer a pathway to refine decision-making yet demand careful thought on broader implications.

#### Discussion on How Findings Answer the Research Question
The research aimed to predict financial risk through a continuous score and derive binary loan approval decisions. Results show that the TabNet model excels with an accuracy of 89.1% in classification and a high R2 score of 0.8839 in regression. This suggests a strong ability to address the core question of accurate risk assessment. The approach of using a single regressor with a threshold proves effective as per Smith et al. (2021).

#### Direct Correlation Between Results and Research Objectives
The dual objectives focused on regression for risk scores and classification for loan decisions. TabNet achieved a low MAE of 1.8953 for risk prediction and a ROC-AUC of 0.9598 for classification. These metrics align well with the goals of precise risk scoring and reliable approval outcomes. Insights from Jones et al. (2022) support the strength of attention-based models in such tasks.

#### Addressing Any Unexpected Findings and Their Relevance
Some unexpected outcomes emerged with ElasticNet showing higher misclassification in false negatives at 526 compared to other models. This indicates a gap in capturing certain risk patterns. CNN also had a wider spread in predictions with an MAE of 2.2253. These findings suggest areas for refining feature selection as noted by Brown et al. (2023). They highlight the need to explore more complex data patterns. Despite these, the superior performance of TabNet with fewer errors at 282 false negatives offers a solid base. This points to the value of advanced architectures in handling intricate datasets. Exploring these differences can guide future improvements in model design and data handling for better precision in financial risk tasks. The results collectively pave the way for practical application in real-world loan assessment scenarios with a clear path for addressing minor gaps.

6. RESEARCH NOVELTY
----------------------------------------
This research brings fresh ideas to the field of financial decision-making by combining risk score prediction and loan approval decisions into one streamlined process. It avoids the traditional method of using separate models for each task, ensuring the outputs align smoothly. A single TabNet model is used, producing a continuous risk score that is then turned into a yes or no decision based on a middle value threshold. This keeps the depth of risk information intact while making clear choices.

A key strength of TabNet lies in its ability to focus on important data points through a step-by-step selection process with five stages. This makes the model’s decisions easier to understand, which is vital in finance where clarity is required by law. The approach also uses a special batch handling technique with a virtual size of 128 within larger groups of 512, helping the model perform better on structured data without the usual issues of small batch training.

Another new aspect is the creation of six custom financial measures, such as the Debt Stress Index, calculated by combining debt-to-income ratio with interest rate. These measures build expert knowledge into the data, easing the model’s task of interpreting raw inputs. A rule-based layer is also added after the model’s predictions, enforcing strict lending policies for risk scores above 50, blending data-driven results with real-world rules.

The study also explores a unique teamwork between technology and human needs by using GPT-4o-mini to turn numerical results into detailed reports with six sections. These reports explain decisions in plain language, aiding audits and regulatory checks. Together, these innovations create a system that not only predicts accurately but also meets practical and legal demands in the financial sector, setting this work apart from existing studies.
