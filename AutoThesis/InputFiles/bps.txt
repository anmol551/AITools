**Summary of Research Pipeline, Data, Accuracy, Novelty, and Future Recommendations**

**Research Pipeline**: The research by Al Bataineh et al. (2025) follows a structured pipeline to develop an AI-generated versus human text detection model. The process begins with data collection, sourcing human-generated text from Medium and Wikipedia articles via Kaggle CLI and Hugging Face datasets, and machine-generated text using DistilGPT-2 with WMT16 and Rotten Tomatoes inputs. Data preprocessing involves cleaning for punctuation, non-ASCII characters, and standardizing word counts (50–450 words) to create a balanced dataset of 10,000 records (5,000 human, 5,000 machine-generated). Feature extraction employs vectorization techniques: Bag of Words (BoW), Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF), BERT embeddings, and neural network embeddings. Models tested include traditional machine learning (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost) and deep learning architectures (LSTM, CNN, CNN-LSTM). Performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score) are evaluated, and SHAP analysis is used for model explainability. The TF-IDF Random Forest model is deployed as a Streamlit web application for user interaction, with code and data shared on GitHub.

**Data Details**: The dataset comprises 10,000 records, equally split between human and machine-generated texts. Human texts are sourced from 185,000 Medium articles (average 834 words) and 19,990 Wikipedia articles (average 3,236 words), cleaned to 5,000 records with an average of 256 words. Machine-generated texts (5,000 records, average 276 words) are produced by DistilGPT-2 using WMT16 (4,533 records) and Rotten Tomatoes (4,665 records) inputs, with a maximum length of 512 tokens. Preprocessing ensures balanced word counts to avoid bias, and the vocabulary size is 82,292 unique terms.

**Accuracy of Methods**: Traditional machine learning models achieved high performance: Logistic Regression scored 0.90 across BoW, TF-IDF, and BERT; Random Forest scored 0.87 (BoW, TF-IDF) and 0.81 (BERT); XGBoost scored 0.91 (BoW, TF-IDF) and 0.83 (BERT). Deep learning models showed: LSTM with 98.75% training and 88.90% validation accuracy; CNN with 98.90% training and 90.80% validation accuracy; CNN-LSTM with 98.76% training and 87.90% validation accuracy.

**Best Algorithm and Accuracy**: The CNN model achieved the highest validation accuracy of 90.80% with TF-IDF representation, training in 9.21 seconds, making it the most efficient and effective model for further optimization.

**Novel Element**: The research introduces a novel, balanced dataset of 10,000 records, combining human and machine-generated texts, publicly available on GitHub. This standardized benchmark, paired with a user-friendly Streamlit web application, enhances accessibility and collaboration in AI text detection research.

**Future Recommendations**: Future work should utilize advanced models like GPT-4 for improved text generation, expand dataset size and diversity to include non-English texts, and automate prompt creation for efficiency. Using pre-ChatGPT Wikipedia articles ensures human authenticity. Faster data collection via bulk API requests and addressing biases in non-native English texts are also recommended to enhance model generalizability and ethical applicability.
